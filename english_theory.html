Parts of Speech – The Comprehensive Guide

This section covers every grammatical unit in the English language, focusing on definitions, classifications, and the specific "exception rules" tested in banking and government exams.

1. The Noun

Definition: A noun is a word used as the name of a person, place, thing, animal, idea, quality, or state of being. It functions as the Subject or Object of a verb.

A. Classification of Nouns

1. Proper Noun

Specific names of particular people, places, or organizations. Always capitalized.

• People: Elon Musk, Shakespeare, Virat Kohli
• Places: London, The Taj Mahal, Mount Everest
• Organizations: RBI, NASA, Google
• Note: Generally, no article (a/an/the) is used before proper nouns, except for specific geographic entities (The Ganges, The USA).
2. Common Noun

General names given to every person or thing of the same class or kind.

• boy, city, planet, company, river, mobile, teacher
3. Collective Noun

Refers to a group of individuals spoken of as a whole.

• People: A team of players, A jury of judges, A crowd of people, An army of soldiers
• Animals: A pride of lions, A flock of sheep, A school of fish, A swarm of bees
• Things: A fleet of ships, A bunch of keys, A library of books

Exam Trap: Collective nouns take a singular verb when acting as a unit, but a plural verb when divided in opinion.

4. Material Noun

Names of substances or matter from which things are made. They are usually uncountable.

• Gold, silver, iron, wood, plastic, milk, cotton, silk
• Note: We do not use "a/an" with material nouns. (Correct: This ring is made of gold.)
5. Abstract Noun

Names of quality, action, or state that cannot be touched or seen, only felt.

• Quality: Honesty, Bravery, Wisdom, Darkness
• Action: Laughter, Theft, Movement, Judgment
• State: Childhood, Poverty, Slavery, Sickness

B. Noun & Number (The Most Tested Area)

Rule 1: Uncountable Nouns (Always Singular)

The following nouns have no plural form. Do not add 's/es'. Do not use 'a/an' before them. Use "much" or "some" with them, not "many".

List: Scenery, Poetry, Furniture, Advice, Information, Hair, Language, Business, Mischief, Bread, Stationery, Crockery, Luggage, Baggage, Postage, Knowledge, Wasting, Jewelry, Breakage, Equipment, Evidence, Work (meaning labor), News.
❌ The sceneries of Kashmir are beautiful.
✅ The scenery of Kashmir is beautiful.
❌ He gave me an advice.
✅ He gave me a piece of advice.
Rule 2: Nouns Plural in Form but Singular in Meaning

These end in 's' but take a singular verb.

• Subjects: Mathematics, Physics, Politics, Economics, Ethics
• Diseases: Measles, Mumps, Rickets
• Games: Billiards, Darts
✅ Mathematics is a difficult subject.
✅ No news is good news.
Rule 3: Nouns Singular in Form but Plural in Meaning

These look singular but always take a plural verb.

List: Cattle, Cavalry, Infantry, Poultry, Peasantry, Children, Gentry, Police, People.
✅ The cattle are grazing in the field. (Not "is")
✅ The police have arrested the thief.

C. The Possessive Case (Apostrophe Rules)

  • Living Things: Add 's (e.g., Ram's book, Cow's tail).
  • Non-Living Things: Use "of" (e.g., The leg of the table - NOT Table's leg).
  • Plural nouns ending in S: Add only apostrophe ' (e.g., Boys' hostel, Girls' college).
  • Compound Nouns: Add 's to the last word (e.g., My sister-in-law's house).
  • Double Apostrophe: Never use two apostrophes together (❌ My wife's secretary's mother → ✅ The mother of my wife's secretary).

2. The Pronoun

Definition: A word used to replace a noun to avoid repetition and improve style.

A. Personal Pronouns

These represent specific people or things. They have three cases:

Person Subjective (Doer) Objective (Receiver) Possessive Adjective Possessive Pronoun
1st (Speaker) I, We Me, Us My, Our Mine, Ours
2nd (Listener) You You Your Yours
3rd (Topic) He, She, It, They Him, Her, It, Them His, Her, Its, Their His, Hers, -, Theirs

B. Important Rules of Personal Pronouns

Rule 1: The Order of Pronouns (2-3-1)

When multiple pronouns act as the subject in a normal/positive sentence, the order is Second Person, Third Person, First Person.

✅ You, he, and I will go to the party. (2-3-1)
❌ I, you, and he will go.

Exception (1-2-3): If the sentence refers to a mistake, sin, or confession, or if the pronouns are plural (we, you, they), use 1st, 2nd, 3rd.

✅ I, you, and he have committed this crime.
Rule 2: Objective Case After Prepositions

After prepositions (between, let, except, but, like), use the Objective Case.

✅ Between you and me. (Not "you and I")
✅ Let him go. (Not "he")
✅ Nobody was present except me.

C. Other Types of Pronouns

1. Reflexive & Emphatic Pronouns (myself, yourself, himself)
  • Reflexive: Action turns back to the subject. (He hurt himself.)
  • Emphatic: Used for emphasis. (I myself did this work.)
  • Trap: Never use a reflexive pronoun as a subject. (❌ Myself is John. → ✅ I am John.)
2. Distributive Pronouns (Each, Either, Neither)

Always singular and take singular verbs.

✅ Either of the two roads leads to the station.
3. Relative Pronouns (Who, Which, That, Whom, Whose)
  • Who: For persons (Subject). The man who called.
  • Whom: For persons (Object). The man whom I met.
  • Which: For animals/things. The book which I lost.
  • That: Can replace who/which; mandatory after superlatives (the best) or words like 'all', 'none', 'nothing'. (All that glitters is not gold.)

3. The Adjective

Definition: A word that qualifies (adds meaning to) a noun or a pronoun.

A. Types of Adjectives

1. Quality: Good, bad, beautiful, tall. (Shows the kind)
2. Quantity: Some, little, enough, much. (Shows how much - for Uncountables)
3. Number: Many, few, five, first. (Shows how many - for Countables)
4. Demonstrative: This, that, these, those.
5. Possessive: My, your, his, her, their.
6. Interrogative: Which, what, whose.

B. Degrees of Comparison

There are three degrees: Positive (tall), Comparative (taller), Superlative (tallest).

Rule 1: Latin Adjectives (The "to" Rule)

Adjectives ending in -ior (Senior, Junior, Superior, Inferior, Prior, Posterior, Anterior) take to instead of than.

✅ He is senior to me. (Not "than me")

Preferable also takes to. (Coffee is preferable to tea.)

Rule 2: Double Comparatives/Superlatives

Never use two comparatives or superlatives together.

❌ He is more taller than you.
✅ He is taller than you.
Rule 3: Order of Adjectives (OSASCOMP)

If multiple adjectives describe one noun, follow this order:

Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Color → Origin → Material → Purpose
✅ A beautiful (Op) large (Si) old (A) round (Sh) brown (C) Indian (Or) wooden (M) table.
Rule 4: Little vs Few
  • Little: (Uncountable) Hardly any (Negative). There is little water.
  • A Little: (Uncountable) Some (Positive). There is a little water.
  • The Little: (Uncountable) Not much, but all that is there.
  • Few: (Countable) Hardly any. Few friends.
  • A Few: (Countable) Some. A few friends.

4. The Verb

Definition: A word used to say something about a person, place, or thing. It is the most essential part of a sentence.

A. Transitive vs Intransitive

• Transitive: Action passes to an Object. (He kicked the ball.) Needs an object to make sense.
• Intransitive: Action stops with the doer. (The baby sleeps.) No object needed.

B. Finite vs Non-Finite Verbs

Finite Verbs: Change form based on subject/tense (e.g., I go, He goes, I went).

Non-Finite Verbs: Do not act as the main verb. They have three types:

1. The Infinitive (To + V1)

Acts as a noun. (To err is human. I want to go.)

2. The Gerund (V1 + ing)

Acts as a Noun (Verbal Noun). Used as a subject or object.

✅ Smoking is injurious to health.
✅ I like reading.

Shortcut: Prepositions are usually followed by a Gerund, not Infinitive. (He is fond of playing cricket.)

3. The Participle

Acts as an Adjective (Verbal Adjective).

• Present Participle (V-ing): A rolling stone. (Active)
• Past Participle (V3): A broken glass. (Completed/Passive)

C. Stative Verbs (No "Ing" Rule)

Certain verbs representing state of mind, senses, or possession are not used in continuous tenses.

List: See, hear, smell, notice, recognize, appear, look, seem, want, wish, desire, feel, love, hate, hope, refuse, prefer, think, suppose, believe, agree, consider, trust, remember, forget, know, understand, imagine, mean, own, possess, belong, contain, consist.
❌ I am loving it. → ✅ I love it.
❌ She is owning a car. → ✅ She owns a car.
❌ I am hearing a noise. → ✅ I hear a noise.

5. The Adverb

Definition: A word that modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. It answers "How?", "When?", "Where?", or "To what extent?".

A. Types of Adverbs

1. Manner: How? (Slowly, bravely, hard, well). He works hard.
2. Time: When? (Yesterday, soon, now, daily).
3. Place: Where? (Here, there, everywhere, up).
4. Frequency: How often? (Always, never, often, seldom).
5. Degree: How much? (Very, too, quite, almost).

B. Important Rules

Rule 1: Adjective vs Adverb

Adjectives qualify nouns; Adverbs qualify verbs. Confusion often happens with "Good" vs "Well".

❌ He plays good. (Good is an adj)
✅ He plays well. (Well is an adverb)
Rule 2: Position of Adverbs (MPFT)

When multiple adverbs are used, the order is: Manner → Place → Frequency → Time.

✅ She sang beautifully (M) at the concert (P) last night (T).
Rule 3: Rule of Inversion

If a sentence starts with a negative adverb (Hardly, Scarcely, No sooner, Seldom, Never), the helping verb comes before the subject.

❌ Hardly he had reached the station...
✅ Hardly had he reached the station...
✅ Seldom does he visit his parents.

6. The Preposition

Definition: A word placed before a noun/pronoun to show its relation to some other word in the sentence.

Common Confusing Pairs

1. Between vs Among
• Between: Used for two persons/things. (Distribute sweets between Ram and Shyam.)
• Among: Used for more than two. (Distribute sweets among the students.)
2. Beside vs Besides
• Beside: By the side of / Next to. (Go and sit beside your father.)
• Besides: In addition to. (Besides English, he knows French.)
3. In vs Into
• In: Position of rest inside something. (He is in the room.)
• Into: Motion inwards. (He jumped into the river.)
4. On vs Upon
• On: Rest on surface. (The book is on the table.)
• Upon: Motion. ( The cat jumped upon the table.)

7. The Conjunction

Definition: A word used to join words, phrases, or clauses.

A. Correlative Conjunctions (Fixed Pairs)

These pairs must always be used together. This is a favorite area for error detection.

1. Either... or: Either come in or go out.
2. Neither... nor: He is neither rich nor poor.
3. Not only... but also: He is not only smart but also hardworking.
4. Both... and: Both Ram and Shyam are present.
5. Though/Although... yet: Though he is poor, yet he is honest. (Use 'yet' or a comma, never 'but').
6. No sooner... than: No sooner did I see him than he ran away. (Note: THAN, not WHEN).
7. Hardly/Scarcely... when: Hardly had I reached when it rained.

B. Important Rules

Rule: Parallelism

Words joined by conjunctions must be of the same rank (Noun-Noun, Adj-Adj, V-ing-V-ing).

❌ He likes swimming and to run.
✅ He likes swimming and running.

8. The Interjection

Definition: Words used to express sudden feelings or emotions. They have no grammatical relation to the rest of the sentence.

• Hurrah! (Joy) - Hurrah! We won.
• Alas! (Sorrow) - Alas! He is dead.
• Bravo! (Approval) - Bravo! Well played.
• Oh! (Surprise/Grief) - Oh! What a shot.

Determiners & Articles – The Zero-Error Guide

Determiners introduce nouns. Articles (A, An, The) are the most important type. This section focuses on the subtle rules of 'Usage' and 'Omission'.

1. Determiners vs. Adjectives

Definition: A determiner is a word that introduces a noun and limits its reference. It always comes before an adjective (if present).

Structure: Determiner + (Adjective) + Noun
• A good boy (A = Determiner)
• My red car (My = Determiner)

Types of Determiners

1. Articles: A, An, The
2. Demonstratives: This, That, These, Those
3. Possessives: My, Our, Your, His, Her, Their
4. Quantifiers: Some, Any, Much, Many, Little, Few
5. Distributives: Each, Every, Either, Neither

2. Quantifiers (The Confusing Pairs)

A. Much vs. Many

• Many: Used with Countable Plural nouns. (Many students, Many books)
• Much: Used with Uncountable nouns. (Much water, Much patience)
Exam Shortcut: "Many a"
If "Many a" is used, the noun and verb must be Singular.
❌ Many a men were present.
✅ Many a man was present.

B. Little vs. Few (High Weightage)

Meaning Uncountable (Quantity) Countable (Number)
Negative (Hardly any) Little
(Little hope left)		 Few
(Few friends visit)
Positive (Some/At least something) A Little
(A little knowledge)		 A Few
(A few days left)
Total (Not much/many, but all there is) The Little
(I drank the little milk left.)		 The Few
(The few clothes I have.)

3. Indefinite Articles (A / An)

Used with singular countable nouns to introduce them for the first time.

The Sound Rule (Crucial)

The choice depends on the sound of the first letter, not the spelling.

Use 'An' (Vowel Sound: A, E, I, O, U):
• An apple
• An honest man (H is silent, 'O' sound)
• An M.L.A. ('Em' starts with A sound)
• An M.P.
• An F.I.R. ('Ef' starts with A sound)
Use 'A' (Consonant Sound):
• A boy
• A university ('Y' sound - Yu-ni-ver-sity)
• A European ('Y' sound)
• A one-rupee coin ('W' sound - Wan)

4. Definite Article (The)

Used for specific, unique, or previously mentioned nouns.

Key Usage Rules

1. Unique Things & Nature
• The Sun, The Earth, The Moon, The Equator.
2. Geographic Entities
• Rivers: The Ganges, The Nile.
• Oceans/Seas: The Pacific, The Red Sea.
• Mountain Ranges (Plural): The Himalayas, The Alps. (BUT: No 'The' for single peaks like Mount Everest).
• Groups of Islands: The West Indies, The Andamans.
3. Countries with "United/Republic" or Plural Names
• The USA, The UK, The UAE, The Netherlands, The Philippines.
• (BUT: No 'The' for India, China, France).
4. Superlatives & Ordinals
• He is the best player.
• This is the most important question.
• The first, The second, The last.
5. The + Adjective = Plural Noun
• The rich = Rich people.
• The poor = Poor people.
✅ The rich are (not is) generally happy.
Advanced Rule: "The" in Comparison
When a selection is made between two.
Structure: The + Comparative + of the two
✅ She is the smarter of the two sisters.

5. Omission of Articles (Where NOT to use)

Most errors in exams come from using articles where they are not needed.

1. Before Proper Nouns

❌ The Delhi is a big city. → ✅ Delhi is a big city.
❌ The Newton was a scientist. → ✅ Newton was a scientist.
(Exception: Comparison - He is the Newton of our age.)

2. Before Languages, Subjects, Games, Meals

❌ He speaks the English. → ✅ He speaks English.
❌ I play the cricket. → ✅ I play cricket.
❌ I have the lunch at 1 PM. → ✅ I have lunch at 1 PM.

3. Before "Primary Purpose" Locations

When visiting these places for their primary purpose, do not use 'The'.

List: School, College, University, Church, Hospital, Bed, Prison, Court.
✅ He goes to school daily. (as a student)
✅ He went to bed at 10 PM. (to sleep)
Exception: If visiting for another reason, use 'The'.
✅ My father went to the school to meet the principal.

4. Before "Man/Woman/Mankind" in General Sense

❌ The man is mortal.
✅ Man is mortal.

5. Before Names of Diseases

❌ The Cancer, The Malaria. → ✅ Cancer, Malaria.
Exceptions: The Measles, The Mumps, The Rickets, The Plague, The Flu.

Subject-Verb Agreement – The 22 Golden Rules

This is the definitive checklist. Do not skip a single rule.

Rules 1–5: Connection Logic

Rule 1: The "Prepositional Phrase" Trap

The verb agrees with the true subject, not the noun in the prepositional phrase (words after 'of', 'in', 'for').

❌ The quality of the mangoes are good.
✅ The quality of the mangoes is good. (Subject is Quality)

Rule 2: The "Additive" Connectors (First Subject Dominates)

If subjects are joined by: As well as, Along with, Together with, With, Besides, Like, Unlike, In addition to, Accompanied by.

✅ The Prime Minister, along with his ministers, is visiting.
✅ My parents, unlike my uncle, are strict.

Rule 3: The "Alternative" Connectors (Nearest Subject Dominates)

If subjects are joined by: Or, Nor, Either...or, Neither...nor, Not only...but also.

✅ Either the father or his sons were punished.
✅ Not only the students but also the teacher is responsible.

Rule 4: The "And" Exception (Same Person/Idea)

Usually "And" = Plural. BUT, if two nouns refer to the same person or a single idea, use Singular.

✅ Bread and butter is my breakfast. (One Dish)
✅ The Collector and District Magistrate is on leave. (One Person holding two titles).
(Contrast: The Collector and the District Magistrate are... = Two people).

Rule 5: Indefinite Pronouns (Always Singular)

Words ending in -one, -body, -thing (Someone, Everybody, Nothing, Anyone) are always singular.

✅ Everyone in the class wants (not want) to succeed.

Rules 6–10: Distributives & Relatives

Rule 6: Each / Every (The Absolute Singular)

If "Each" or "Every" appears before a subject, the verb is ALWAYS singular, even if joined by "and".

✅ Every man and every woman has (not have) a vote.
✅ Each minute and each second is precious.

Rule 7: Either of / Neither of (The Selection)

Structure: Either/Neither/Each/One + OF + Plural Noun + Singular Verb.

❌ One of the boys are absent.
✅ One of the boys is absent.
✅ Neither of the two candidates was suitable.

Rule 8: Relative Pronoun (Who/Which/That)

The verb after a relative pronoun agrees with the Antecedent (the noun immediately before it).

✅ It is I who am to blame. (Antecedent is I)
✅ It is he who is responsible.

Rule 9: The "One of the... Who" Exception

Structure: One of the + Plural Noun + Who + Plural Verb.

✅ He is one of the doctors who are treating COVID patients.
(Logic: Many doctors are treating; he is one of them).

Rule 10: The "ONLY One of the... Who" Exception

Structure: The ONLY one of the + Plural Noun + Who + Singular Verb.

✅ He is the only one of the doctors who is available.

Rules 11–15: Nouns & Numbers

Rule 11: Uncountable Nouns (Always Singular)

Scenery, Furniture, Advice, Information, Luggage, Stationery, Poetry.

✅ The scenery of Switzerland enchants me.

Rule 12: Plural Form, Singular Meaning

Physics, Mathematics, News, Ethics, Innings, Measles, Mumps.

✅ Physics is my favorite subject.

Rule 13: Singular Form, Plural Meaning

Cattle, Police, People, Children, Infantry, Cavalry.

❌ The cattle is grazing.
✅ The cattle are grazing.

Rule 14: Fixed Pairs (Always Plural)

Scissors, Trousers, Spectacles, Binoculars, Tongs. (Things with two parts).

✅ My shoes are new.
✅ A pair of shoes is new. (If 'Pair' is used, it becomes singular).

Rule 15: A Number vs The Number

• A number of = Many (Plural Verb)
• The number of = A count (Singular Verb)

✅ A number of students were absent.
✅ The number of students was twenty.

Rules 16–22: Advanced Triggers

Rule 16: Units of Measurement

Distance, Money, Time, Weight as a collective unit = Singular.

✅ Ten thousand rupees is a good salary.

Rule 17: Fractions & Percentages

The verb depends on the Noun after 'OF'.

✅ Half of the apple is rotten.
✅ Half of the apples are rotten.

Rule 18: "More than one"

Structure: More than one + Singular Noun + Singular Verb.

✅ More than one man was killed.

Rule 19: "More ... than one"

Structure: More + Plural Noun + than one + Plural Verb.

✅ More men than one were killed.

Rule 20: Imaginary Sentences (Subjunctive)

In sentences expressing a wish or impossible condition (If, As if, I wish), use WERE for all subjects.

✅ I wish I were the PM.
✅ If he were here, he would help.

Rule 21: "Nothing But"

Followed by Noun (Singular/Plural) → Always Singular Verb.

✅ Nothing but books is sold here.
(The subject is 'Nothing', not 'books').

Rule 22: The "Adjective as Noun" Rule

The + Adjective (representing a class) → Plural Verb.

✅ The Rich are not always happy.
✅ The Injured were taken to the hospital.

3. Tenses & Conditional Sentences – The Complete Manual

This section covers the 12 Tenses and 4 Conditional structures in microscopic detail. It focuses on "Time Markers" (Signal Words) which are the keys to solving Error Detection questions.

1. The Present Tense

A. Simple Present (Present Indefinite)

Definition: Used for actions that are factual, habitual, or scheduled. It does not indicate if the action is happening right now.

Structure: Subject + V1 (+ s/es for 3rd Person Singular)
• He plays. / They play.
1. Usage: Habitual Actions
• He smokes.
• I get up at 6 AM.
2. Usage: Universal/General Truths
• The sun rises in the East.
• Water boils at 100°C.
• Fortune favors the brave.
3. Usage: Scheduled Future (Official)

For official timetables/programs, use Simple Present instead of Future.

• The Prime Minister visits America next month.
• The college reopens in October.
Signal Words (The Clues):
Always, Often, Usually, Generally, Daily, Every day/week, Seldom, Never, Hardly, Scarcely, Once a week.

B. Present Continuous

Definition: Used for an action going on at the time of speaking or a temporary situation.

Structure: Subject + is/am/are + V1 + ing
Signal Words:
Now, At present, At this moment, These days, Currently, Nowadays.
🚨 EXAM TRAP: The Stative Verbs (No -ING)
Certain verbs describing state, possession, or feeling are NEVER used in Continuous tense.

List: See, Hear, Smell, Notice, Recognize, Appear, Look, Seem, Want, Wish, Desire, Feel, Love, Hate, Hope, Refuse, Prefer, Think, Suppose, Believe, Agree, Consider, Trust, Remember, Forget, Know, Understand, Mean, Own, Possess, Belong, Contain, Consist.

❌ I am knowing him.
✅ I know him.
❌ She is owning a car.
✅ She owns a car.
❌ I am hearing a noise.
✅ I hear a noise.

C. Present Perfect

Definition: A mix of past and present. The action is complete, but the effect is still on the present. Time of action is NOT specified.

Structure: Subject + has/have + V3
Signal Words:
Just, Already, Yet, So far, Ever, Never, Recently, Lately, Up to now.
Exam Examples:
• I have seen the Taj Mahal. (Experience exists now)
• He has just finished his work.
• He has not come yet. (Note: 'Yet' is usually used in negative sentences).

D. Present Perfect Continuous

Definition: Action started in the past and is still continuing in the present.

Structure: Subject + has/have + been + V1 + ing
Rule: Since vs. For
• Since (Point of Time): Since Monday, Since 1995, Since 2 PM, Since birth.
• For (Period of Time): For 2 days, For 5 years, For a long time.
✅ It has been raining since morning.
✅ She has been sleeping for 5 hours.

2. The Past Tense

A. Simple Past (Past Indefinite)

Definition: Action completed in the past at a specific time.

Structure: Subject + V2 (Did + V1 in negative/question)
🚨 CRITICAL TRAP: Simple Past vs Present Perfect
If a sentence mentions a Past Time (Yesterday, Last Year, Ago, In 1947), you MUST use Simple Past.

❌ I have bought a car yesterday. (Wrong because 'Yesterday' is present)
✅ I bought a car yesterday.
✅ I have bought a car. (Correct if time is not mentioned).
Special Rule: "It is time"

If the phrase It is time / It is high time / It is about time is followed by a subject, use V2 (Past).

✅ It is high time we studied. (Not 'study' or 'should study').

B. Past Continuous

Definition: Action that was in progress at a specific moment in the past.

Structure: Subject + was/were + V1 + ing
• I was watching TV when he called.
• While she was dancing, she fell.

C. Past Perfect (The Past of the Past)

Definition: Used ONLY when two actions happened in the past. It shows which one happened first.

Formula:
1st Action (Earlier) → Had + V3
2nd Action (Later) → V2 (Simple Past)
Signal Words: Before, After, By the time.
• The train had left (1st) before I reached (2nd) the station.
• I reached (2nd) the station after the train had left (1st).
Trap: Never use Past Perfect for a single action.
❌ I had seen him yesterday.
✅ I saw him yesterday.

D. Past Perfect Continuous

Definition: Action started in the past, continued for a time, and ended in the past.

• He had been waiting for two hours when the bus finally came.

3. The Future Tense

A. Simple Future

Definition: Action that will happen in time to come.

Structure: Subject + will/shall + V1
• I will help you.
• He will come tomorrow.

B. Future Continuous

Definition: Action that will be in progress at a specific future time.

• At this time tomorrow, I will be flying to London.

C. Future Perfect (The Deadline Tense)

Definition: Action that will be completed by a certain time in the future.

Structure: Subject + will have + V3
Signal Words: By, By the time, By the end of.
• By next year, I will have finished my graduation.
• By the time you reach, he will have left.

D. Future Perfect Continuous

Definition: Action that will continue up to a point in the future.

• By 2030, we will have been living here for 20 years.

Major Exam Traps in Conditionals

1. No Future Tense in 'If' Clause

Never use will, shall, would immediately after If, When, Unless, Until, As soon as.

❌ If he will come, I will help him.
✅ If he comes, I will help him.
2. The "Were" Rule (Type 2)

In imaginary sentences, use WERE for all subjects (I, He, She, It).

❌ If I was a bird...
✅ If I were a bird...
3. Inversion (Removing 'If')

You can remove 'If' by placing the helping verb before the subject.

• Type 2: Were I a bird, I would fly. (= If I were a bird)
• Type 3: Had I seen him, I would have stopped car. (= If I had seen him)
